Course Description

This course will introduce students to the expansive world of anthropology, with an emphasis on the four fields that define the discipline: cultural anthropology/ethnography, archaeology, physical anthropology, and linguistics (to a limited extent). Students will be learn how to use the tools of anthropology to examine some of the key assumptions about what makes us human. How, for instance, does the process of enculturation - the act of teaching a child its cultural over its life course - shape one's personality, actions, and worldview? What aspects of our personalities are formed at birth, and what are learned (also known as the nature vs. nurture debate)?

Lectures in the course will serve as a supplement and backdrop to the texts assigned, offering contextual information on the methodical, interpretative, and theoretical perspectives presented in the course texts. Thus, to succeed in this course, students must complete the reading and be ready to discuss it in class if called upon. Students will be asked to interrogate and question the text's's arguments by examining the data presented and collected by the text's author. Discussion that is not grounded in scientific reasoning will be curtailed to make room for conversations grounded in the texts and data presented in lectures.

We will use a Google Document to comment and keep track of important keywords and concepts during the lecture. Please click here to access the document. You must have a Google account to edit the document. Students are encouraged to add questions to the document as lecture is taking place. Dr. Camp or her teaching assistant will answer questions or clarify concepts during the class or after class.

Grading

This course is graded on a cumulative 200 point scale. Below is a breakdown of the points for this course.

Quizzes will be held in-class and will cover the content leading up to the day of the quiz. No study guides will be provided for quizzes, which is why it is essential you attend every class, read the course texts, watch the course films, and take extensive notes.

There will be 3 Metacognition assignments due throughout the course. Metacognitions will comprise of a writing prompt asking you to reflect upon some of the current course content. Metacognitions will be handed back with feedback from the teaching assistant or Dr. Camp. Each metacognition is worth 10 points for a total of 30 points throughout the semester. Metacognition assignments should demonstrate a strong command over the course lecture content, course readings, and, when applicable, course films. These are not meant to be "opinion" pieces, but rather sustained, thoughtful, and substantial engagements with the course literature and lecture topics.

The Final Examination will be held in-class. Please plan your travel accordingly, as there will be no final makeups. A study guide will be provided for the final. A final review session will be held on the last day of the course; please bring your questions to that class rather than emailing them to the course instructor or teaching assistant. The final is comprehensive, meaning that it will cover the entire semester's worth of content. Students are encouraged to form small study groups well in advance of the final examination. Please make sure to bring your own blue book for the examination. Note notes, phones, computers, or other materials will be permitted in the final: please bring your bluebook and a pencil. Final examinations will not be returned to students, but students are welcome to view their graded exam in Dr. Camp's office.

Academic Dishonesty

Plagiarism of any form will not be tolerated. Plagiarism, in its simplest form, involves copying someone else’s work and claiming it as your own. I also count plagiarizing your own work from another course and turning it in as if it were new, original work in my class as plagiarism. To avoid university penalties, always make sure to properly cite your sources, including books, websites, journal articles, unpublished materials, and discussions with your classmates. Please review the university’s policy on academic dishonesty and citation rules before completing your first written assignment in class. These guidelines can be found here.

Phone calls, texting, and use of cellular phones, headsets, MP3 players, and i-Pods during class is prohibited as it detracts from the learning environment. University Learning Outcomes

This section outlines how the course content, assignments, and objectives align with the University of Idaho’s Learning Outcomes, that can be accessed here.

1. Learn and Integrate. Students will read texts that bridge a variety of interdisciplinary fields beyond anthropology, including medicine, religious studies, gender studies, international studies, and heritage management. Students will learn be asked to reflect upon how these bodies of scholarship relates to their own career paths and lives.

2. Think and Create. Students will learn how to separate fiction from fact through the process of analyzing texts and cross-checking secondary sources.

3. Communicate. Students will be required to submit assignments that demonstrate their ability to communicate through the use of the written word. These assignments consist of metacognitions.

4. Clarify Purpose and Perspective. Students will be expect to reflect upon their own life experiences and deeply held beliefs while exploring different ways of thinking about, explaining, and interpreting other people’s ways of living and belief systems. They will be asked to reevaluate their own cultural perspectives on “hot” topics such as religion, gender roles, war, mental illness, parenting, class, inequality, and sexuality.

5. Practice Citizenship. This course encourages an appreciation of the diverse ways people live across the world through course readings and lectures that require students to compare and contrast their own life experiences with that of people outside of the Western world.
