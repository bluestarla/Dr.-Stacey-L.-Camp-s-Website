DR. STACEY L. CAMP is an Associate Professor of Anthropology and the Director of the Alfred W. Bowers Laboratory of Anthropology at the University of Idaho. She completed her B.A. in both Anthropology and English & Comparative Studies at Occidental College in 2001, and finished her Ph.D. in Anthropology at Stanford University in 2009 under the supervision of her doctoral advisor, Dr. Barbara Voss, and committee members Dr. Ian Hodder, Dr. Paulla Ebron, and Dr. Lynn Meskell. She got her first taste of historical archaeology as a field school student of Dr. Charles E. Orser, Jr.'s archaeological project in Strokestown, Ireland in 1999, and has continued to develop her knowledge in the field since then.

At its broadest, her work examines the materiality of immigrants in the late 19th and 20th century Western United States. Her book, The Archaeology of Citizenship (University Press of Florida, 2013), examines the ways marginalized groups managed to cope with isolation, racism, sexism, and segregation through their creative use of material culture. Her publications explore the intersection of race, gender, generation, citizenship, and class within the context of Mexican migrant workers in historic Los Angeles, and, more recently, within incarceration camps that imprisoned Japanese Americans during World War II. She is currently working on a materials-based historical archaeology textbook under advance contract with University Press of Florida. Some of her work can be found on her Academia.edu profile.



Inspired by research during her sabbatical in 2014 and selection to be a funded participant in the National Endowment for the Humanities Institute on Digital Archaeology Method & Practice at Michigan State University, her current work also harnesses digital tools and technologies to better interpret, share, and research archaeological and archival data. She has spent the last two years dabbling in using both open-source GIS software, such as CartoDB, and ESRI products (e.g. ArcMap) to visualize and interpret archaeological data; using game development software, such as Unity and associated applications to create "serious" games about historical events and archaeological sites; creating open-source archaeological databases for researchers, scholars, and the public; exploring new ways of telling stories about data in the digital realm, such as Scalar; learning new content management systems to house culturally sensitive artifactual and archival data, such as Mukurtu; coding websites and using social media to publicize public outreach events and holdings at the University of Idaho's Bowers Laboratory of Anthropology. In the not so distant future, she desires to use some of this parsed-together knowledge to ensure archaeological collections are accessible to the stakeholder groups and communities most passionate about them.




Dr. Camp's family are natives of the Pacific Northwest, and her grandmother, who is 103 years of age, grew up in Black Diamond, Washington: a coal mining town outside of Seattle. Outside of academia, she is involved with the Girl Scouts, figure skating, yoga, and her family.
